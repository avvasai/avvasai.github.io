A Novel Accordion-Like Disk Resonator Gyroscope

MEMS Gyroscope Design · Simulation · Fabrication · Testing — M2D2 Laboratory, Indian Institute of Science

Disk Resonator Gyroscopes sense rotation through Coriolis forces on a vibrating ring. This project tested whether an "accordion-like" ring geometry could beat four established DRG topologies on mechanical sensitivity, and carried the winning design from simulation through fabrication and physical testing.

12.1 µm/rad/s Mechanical sensitivity — 3–4× prior designs
6.77 kHz Drive-mode eigenfrequency — matched within 1.2–1.4% in test
181.6 Q factor Highest of the five topologies tested
46.4% ΔC Largest relative sense-capacitance change

Problem Statement

Existing DRG topologies — Boeing, Honeycomb, Cobweb, and Gear DRG — each pushed sensitivity a little further (0.04 down to 0.015 deg/sec) but always traded electrode placement against mechanical performance. The open question: could a different ring geometry raise mechanical sensitivity without giving up noise performance?

Methodology

Finite-element simulation to select the best geometry, then fabrication and physical testing of the result.

Design & Simulation

Eigenfrequency analysis across five ring topologies (DRG, HDRG, CDRG, GDRG, and the proposed ADRG), each built with 9 rings, 8 mm outer diameter, 10 µm ring width, 60 µm thickness. Capacitance modeling accounted for fringing fields rather than the textbook parallel-plate formula.

Fabrication

Fabricated at CeNSE (Centre for Nano Science and Engineering) and wire-bonded into a 68-pin ceramic package, with color-coded leads carrying drive, sense, and common signals to the test board. Photos and video are in the gallery below.

Test Setup

16 individually addressable outer electrodes handle drive and sense, sharing 4 common pins; a companion accelerometer die on the same package carries separate X/Y sense and feedback pairs. Testing used a Laser Doppler Vibrometer — an AC+DC drive signal excited the resonator while the LDV measured out-of-plane velocity at 0° and 180° to trace the frequency response.

Results

Under controlled conditions (1 atm, 25°C) the fabricated ADRG resonated at ≈6.8 kHz — within 1.2–1.4% of the FEM-predicted eigenfrequency, confirming the simulation held up in silicon.
