Project · Haptics

Knee Instability Correction Device

Wearable haptic brace providing directional vibrotactile feedback for ACL rehabilitation — Johns Hopkins University | 535.691 Haptic Interface Design | Guided by Prof. Jeremy Brown

Duration: Aug 2024 – Dec 2024

Role: Hardware Lead: Designed electronics architecture, fabricated sensor enclosures and motor mounts, integrated flex sensors and ERM array achieving 100% front-bend detection accuracy

Knee instability from ACL injuries affects 80,000-120,000 people annually in the US, with current rehabilitation methods risking muscle atrophy from over-bracing or re-injury from premature free movement. The Knee Instability Correction Device (KICD) bridges this gap using vibrotactile feedback to guide users toward stable knee positions without physically constraining movement.

As Hardware Lead, I designed and built the complete wearable system: three flex sensors in 3D-printed TPU enclosures detect knee angle across sagittal, abduction, and adduction planes, while six ERM motors arranged in a 40mm-spaced array deliver directional 'tracing' vibrations that intuitively guide the user back to stability. Testing with 30+ subjects demonstrated 100% accuracy for front-bend detection and successful directional perception in the majority of users.

Problem Statement

ACL injuries affect 80,000-120,000 people annually in the US, causing knee instability where the knee buckles toward the midline (dynamic valgus) during weight acceptance. Current rehabilitation uses passive bracing that causes muscle atrophy, or free movement that risks re-injury. The challenge: could a wearable haptic device provide a middle ground using vibrotactile feedback to virtually limit dangerous movements while allowing physical freedom for strength development?

Methodology

The project followed a comprehensive design-fabricate-test workflow integrating mechanical design, electronics development, and human perception studies.

Design & Fabrication

The KICD consists of two bands worn above and below the knee forming a soft brace base. Three 1-axis silicone elastomer flex sensors, housed in 3D-printed TPU enclosures, detect knee angle across sagittal, abduction, and adduction planes. Six 3.3V 80mA ERM coin motors (1020 type) are mounted with 40mm spacing in 3D-printed PLA mounts on the upper knee band, providing vibration frequencies from 79-183 Hz.

Electronics Architecture

The system uses an Arduino Mega as the main controller, commanding all ERM motors and receiving data from ELEGOO Nano boards that interface with each flex sensor. Motors are driven through 2N2221A NPN transistors connected to 5V, controlled via PWM to overcome microcontroller current limitations. Two perfboards with connectorized bundles form the physical harness for serviceability.

Control Logic

When knee angle exceeds thresholds (5° for side sensors, 8° for front sensor), directional vibration feedback activates. The vibration pattern starts at the instability direction and traces toward stability, creating an intuitive 'follow me' sensation. Motors activate in a staggered running pattern until the knee returns to neutral position. Automatic calibration (5-10 seconds) accounts for individual leg geometry.

Test Setup

Perception Studies: Preliminary vibrotactile perception studies determined optimal design parameters. Two-point discrimination testing across 4 knee regions with 4 subjects established 40mm motor spacing for ≥75% discrimination accuracy. Intensity perception testing with 9 subjects characterized the nonlinear relationship between PWM duty cycle and perceived intensity, following a 2nd-order polynomial (R²=0.999).

Device Validation: Device performance was validated through testing on 2 individuals with uninjured knees across varying ranges of motion (peak abduction angle 22±11°), followed by haptic demo sessions with 30+ subjects of varying size and gender using a haptic twin configuration for safe manipulation.

Results

Key Findings:

  • 100% accuracy for front knee bend detection and haptic triggering
  • 50% accuracy for compounded abduction direction (requires per-user calibration tuning)
  • 30+ subjects tested in haptic demo sessions with majority successfully perceiving directional feedback
  • ≥75% two-point discrimination accuracy achieved above 24mm spacing, near-perfect above 40mm
  • Intensity perception follows 2nd-order polynomial curve (R²=0.999) with saturation at PWM 225-255
  • 6% of subjects experienced knismesis (tickling sensation) preventing response
  • Vibration perceivable through clothing with no significant damping effect on discrimination

Future Work: Replace ERM motors with voice coils for independent frequency and amplitude modulation; upgrade to 2-axis flex sensors for accurate compound bend detection; redesign TPU mounts with accordion-style walls for multi-plane flexibility; conduct formal user study with motor learning evaluation protocol.
