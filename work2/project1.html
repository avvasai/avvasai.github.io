Project · MEMS

MEMS Inertial Measurement Unit

Designing high-precision MEMS accelerometer and gyroscope for space navigation through parametric optimization and multiphysics simulation — M2D2 Laboratory, Indian Institute of Science | In collaboration with ISRO | Guided by Prof. G K Ananthasuresh

Duration: Jun 2022 – Jul 2024

Over a two-year collaboration between IISc and ISRO, this project aimed to develop a complete MEMS Inertial Measurement Unit for space navigation applications. The IMU comprised two core sensors: a dual-axis capacitive accelerometer and an Accordion-like Disk Resonator Gyroscope (ADRG). Starting from an existing ring-and-spoke gyroscope concept, extensive parametric optimization was performed on both devices using COMSOL multiphysics simulations. Both devices were successfully fabricated at CeNSE, with the gyroscope validated via laser Doppler vibrometry achieving <5% eigenfrequency error. The gyroscope work was published at ICMNS 2024.

Problem Statement

ISRO required high-precision inertial sensors for space navigation applications. Existing DRG topologies — standard DRG, Honeycomb DRG (HDRG), Concentric DRG (CDRG), and Gear-tooth DRG (GDRG) — each advanced sensitivity incrementally but always traded electrode placement against mechanical performance. The challenge: could a novel ring geometry significantly improve mechanical sensitivity and quality factor without compromising noise performance or manufacturability?

Methodology

The project followed a comprehensive design-simulate-fabricate-test workflow spanning both accelerometer and gyroscope development.

Design & Simulation

Accelerometer Design: The MEMS accelerometer employs a proof mass suspended by folded-beam flexures with interdigitated comb-drive electrodes for differential capacitive sensing. A comprehensive parametric study varied beam width (5, 6, 7, 8 µm) and out-of-plane thickness (10, 20, 30, 60 µm) to optimize eigenfrequency (~3 kHz target), displacement sensitivity, capacitance sensitivity (ΔC/g), cross-axis sensitivity, quality factor, and Brownian noise equivalent acceleration.

Gyroscope Topology Comparison: Five DRG topologies were evaluated using COMSOL Multiphysics. The novel Accordion-like DRG (ADRG) introduces accordion-shaped spoke connections that increase mechanical compliance while maintaining structural integrity. All designs used standardized parameters for fair comparison: 9 rings, 8 mm outer diameter, 10 µm ring width, 60 µm thickness, and 0.5 anchor-to-outer-radius ratio.

Eigenfrequency studies identified drive and sense mode frequencies, with mode matching critical for gyroscope sensitivity. Capacitance simulations modeled multi-electrode configurations with fringing field corrections.

Fabrication

Mask Layout Generation: Once mechanical and electrode parameters were finalized through simulation, designs were converted into semiconductor fabrication mask layouts. CleWin was used for initial layout design and layer definition, while KLayout provided advanced editing and DRC (Design Rule Check) verification.

SOI Microfabrication: Devices were fabricated at the Centre for Nano Science and Engineering (CeNSE) at IISc using SOI-based surface micromachining. The fabrication process yielded multiple dies of each sensor type with 60 µm device thickness and 10 µm ring width.

Wire Bonding: Wire bonding connected the MEMS die pads to the PCB carrier for electrical interfacing. Custom pinout schematics were designed specifying electrode-to-pin mappings: the gyroscope required 16 individually addressed outer electrodes plus 4 common connections.

Test Setup

The fabricated ADRG was characterized using a Polytec MSA-500 Micro System Analyzer (Laser Doppler Vibrometer) at atmospheric pressure and room temperature (25°C). The device was electrostatically driven with AC+DC excitation applied to opposing drive electrodes (0° and 180° phase). Frequency sweeps measured the mechanical frequency response, with the resonance peak identifying the eigenfrequency.

Results

Under controlled conditions (1 atm, 25°C) the fabricated ADRG resonated at 6.8 kHz — within <5% of the FEM-predicted eigenfrequency of 6.771 kHz (approximately 0.4% error), confirming the simulation methodology held up in silicon.

Key Findings:

  • ADRG achieved highest quality factor (181.55k) among five DRG topologies tested
  • ADRG demonstrated best relative sense capacitance (46.44% ΔC₀/C₀)
  • Mechanical sensitivity of 12.1×10⁻⁶ µm/rad/s — 3-4× higher than prior designs
  • LDV validation showed <5% eigenfrequency error between simulation and measurement

Future Work: Complete accelerometer characterization using shake table, implement closed-loop electronic readout circuits, vacuum packaging to achieve higher Q factors (targeting >500k), and integrate both sensors into single IMU package with ASIC readout.
