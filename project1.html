Project

A Novel Accordion-Like Disk Resonator Gyroscope

MEMS Gyroscope Design · Simulation · Fabrication · Testing — M2D2 Laboratory, Indian Institute of Science

Project Overview

Disk Resonator Gyroscopes (DRGs) sense rotation using Coriolis forces acting on a vibrating ring — the same physics behind a Foucault pendulum, just shrunk down to millimeters. Over the years, designs like the Boeing DRG, Honeycomb DRG, Cobweb DRG, and Gear DRG each pushed sensitivity a little further, from 0.04 deg/sec down to 0.015 deg/sec, but each traded off electrode placement against mechanical performance.

This project, presented at the ICMNSS Conference under the guidance of Prof. G. K. Ananthasuresh, asked a simple question: could an entirely different ring geometry — an "accordion-like" pattern — beat the existing field on mechanical sensitivity and noise?

Design & Simulation

I ran finite-element eigenfrequency analysis across five ring topologies — DRG, HDRG, CDRG, GDRG, and my proposed ADRG — each built with 9 rings, an 8mm outer diameter, 10µm in-plane ring width, and 60µm thickness. The accordion pattern showed a distinctly lower drive-mode eigenfrequency (6771 Hz) compared to the 11,000–15,000 Hz range of the others.

The last row is the headline: the ADRG's mechanical sensitivity comes out roughly 3–4x higher than every other topology tested, while holding competitive noise performance. Capacitance modeling (accounting for fringing fields, not just the textbook parallel-plate formula) backed this up — the ADRG's relative sense capacitance change was the largest of the five designs at 46.4%, versus 14–29% for the others.

Fabrication

The design moved from simulation to silicon at CeNSE (Centre for Nano Science and Engineering). The photos below show the finished dies — individual ADRG chips on glass slides, each with the resonator's accordion-style ring pattern visible under its protective coating — and the completed chip wire-bonded into a 68-pin ceramic package with color-coded leads carrying drive, sense, and common signals to the test board.

Pinout & Test Setup

The gyroscope's electrode layout uses 4 common pins and 16 individually addressable outer electrodes for drive and sense, while a companion accelerometer die on the same package uses separate X/Y sense and feedback electrode pairs. For testing, I used a Laser Doppler Vibrometer (LDV) setup: a drive signal (combining AC and DC bias) excited the resonator while the LDV measured out-of-plane velocity at 0° and 180° points on the ring to characterize the frequency response.

Results

Under controlled atmospheric conditions (1 atm, 25°C), the fabricated ADRG resonated at approximately 6.8 kHz — within 1.2–1.4% of the FEM-predicted eigenfrequency, confirming the simulation model held up in the real device.

My Contributions

I led the eigenfrequency and capacitance modeling across all five DRG topologies, designed the ADRG geometry, and ran the LDV-based experimental characterization of the fabricated device.
